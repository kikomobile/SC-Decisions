Voting Patterns in the Philippine Supreme Court

Building tools for legal research — breaking down Supreme Court decisions, extracting how each justice votes on each case, and analyzing the patterns.

By Eugenio Maceda Leynes

A decade ago, I spent months of law school writing Python code to scrape, parse, and analyze 35,000 Philippine Supreme Court decisions. This year, I rebuilt most of the workflow in 3 weeks using AI tools. In this piece, I will share what I found: the original research revisited with a fresh set of eyes, new insights from the years of data that have since accumulated, and some reflections on rebuilding this workflow with AI tools.

I have always been interested in work that sits at the intersection of scientific methodology and legal analysis, and I aimed to introduce computational techniques (drawn from my Physics background) as a quantitative complement to traditional doctrinal legal research. One of the questions I wanted to answer was: is there a way to quantitatively analyze voting patterns of justices and detect any influence?

The Court and its Presidents

Supreme Court Composition

The Philippine Supreme Court is composed of 15 Justices, deciding on cases either as a division (composed of 3, 5 or 7 justices) or en banc (all 15 justices). They have tenure until the mandatory retirement age of 70 years old. Once a position opens, justices are appointed by the President of the Philippines, from a pool of candidates chosen by a separate constitutional-created body (the Judicial and Bar Council). This appointing power of the President has always been a point of inquiry on how the court could be affected by the executive’s influence. This is especially true due to the court’s history during the martial law era of former President Ferdinand Marcos.

Timeline scope

Prior to 1986, the Philippines was ruled by a dictator (Ferdinand Marcos) for 21 years. He was able to pack the court with his appointees. In 1986, he was ousted through the People Power Revolution and was replaced by Corazon Aquino. The new President reorganized the entire 15-member court (with a few justices from the Marcos era that were reappointed). This creates a natural and interesting starting point in the history of the Court.

Presidential Terms

Since 1987, Presidents are elected for a single term of six years. However, there are three outliers:

  • Corazon Aquino. As her ascent to Presidency was prior to the 1987 Constitution, she initially held the position of President from February of 1986. In comparison, the term under the 1987 Constitution starts on June 30 following a May election. As such, Corazon Aquino’s term has an extra 4 months.
  • Joseph Estrada. In January of 2001, following a highly contested impeachment case and a peaceful revolution, Joseph Estrada was ousted from the presidency less than 3 years into his term. This elevated his vice-president, Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo.
  • Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo. After serving the remainder of Estrada’s term, she was declared the winner of a separate 6-year term. This resulted in her holding the office for over 9 years and 5 months.

Decisions and Opinions

Cases are decided by majority vote — for en banc cases, typically 8 of 15 justices must agree on the outcome. While the result is often binary (e.g., granting or denying a petition), justices can express their position in several ways:

  • Main decision: One justice (the ponente) writes the ruling and reasoning of the majority.
  • Separate concurring opinion: A justice agrees with the outcome but writes separately to add their own reasoning or emphasis.
  • Separate dissenting opinion: A justice disagrees with the outcome and explains why they would have ruled differently.

A justice who agrees without writing separately is simply counted as a concurring vote. For this research, I treated any separately written opinion as a signal of disagreement with the main decision’s reasoning. Hereinafter, these are referred to as a “dissent”.

From Decisions to Data

I had to (1) collect all the cases, publicly available in the Supreme Court’s e-library in the form of PDF files (746 volumes, each covering between 40–80 cases), (2) extract the texts from the PDF files (some files are searchable, others are scans), (3) breakdown the texts into individual cases (comprising over 35,000 cases), (4) parse the text to get the main decision writer, concurring justices, dissenting justices, and justices with separate opinions, and (5) perform various quantitative analyses on the resulting database.

More details of this workflow are discussed in the appendix.

Measuring Dissent Rate

I took all the cases from 1986 to 2024 and extracted the cases that were decided en banc (for a total of 6,144 cases). From this sub-group, I calculated the number of cases wherein at least 1 justice disagrees with the main decision. For clarity, I took disagreement as when a justice writes a separate opinion, regardless of if the opinion is concurring or dissenting. I divided this by the total number of cases. This calculation is done per year and graphed over time.

Dissent Rate(year) = No. of EN BANC cases with at least one separate opinion Total EN BANC cases × 100

For example: For the window of 7 January 1986 to 7 January 1987, there were 90 cases decided en banc. Of the 90 cases, there were 3 cases that had a separate opinion. The dissent rate for 1986 was calculated to be 3.30% (3 / 90).

Dissent Rate(1986) = 3 90 × 100 = 3.30%

It’s worth noting that the dissent rate moves with both its numerator and denominator. For example, if the number of dissents stays constant, a smaller number of en banc cases can inflate the rate, while a larger number can suppress it. Conversely, if the number of en banc cases stays constant, a higher number of dissents will raise the rate, while a lower number will reduce it.

C. Aquino and Ramos eras had 10% and 12% average dissent rates, respectively. This dropped to 5.1% during Estrada’s era.

Notably, even though the average number of dissent cases during Estrada’s era stayed roughly the same as his predecessors’, the total number of en banc cases more than doubled, which drove the rate down. This indicates a significant increase in incoming cases, the majority of which were decided unanimously by the court.

In contrast, the Arroyo years saw a drop in dissent cases (down to 9.1 per year) despite a similar en banc caseload to Estrada’s era. The Arroyo era is worth a closer look because dissents show a high spread across it.

The middle of her term showed years with less than 5 dissents per year, while the start and end of her term showed a significant number of cases with dissents (between 10 and 21 dissents per year).

Under B. Aquino III, the dissent rate rose to 6.5% — driven mainly by a drop in total en banc cases (down to 127 per year) rather than a significant change in dissents (8.5 per year).

During Duterte’s and Marcos Jr.’s eras, the averages of dissent cases and rates drop significantly — 2.8 and 3.5 dissent cases per year, with average rates of 2.3% and 2.9% respectively. Note that Marcos Jr.’s figures cover a partial term and may shift as more data accumulates.

A Closer Look at Two Eras

Two eras stand out in the numbers above: the terms of Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo and Rodrigo Duterte.

Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo’s ascent to the presidency allowed her an unusually long term of 9 years and 5 months. Through the length of her term and the mandatory retirement age of Supreme Court Justices, she was able to appoint 14 out of the 15 justices of the court (the 15th justice was Reynato Puno, who was originally appointed by Fidel V. Ramos, but was elevated by Arroyo to the position of Chief Justice).

Duterte achieved a similar concentration — 13 of the 15 justices — during a standard six-year term. Twelve of the justices retired during his term. An additional position was deemed vacant after the controversial removal of the Supreme Court Chief Justice Maria Lourdes Sereno via a quo warranto petition (as opposed to impeachment proceedings).

This contrasts with Benigno Aquino III and Fidel V. Ramos, who were able to nominate only 6 justices during their terms, and Joseph Estrada, who was able to nominate 6 justices in less than 3 years as President. These presidents — each with limited appointing power — presided over much higher dissent rates.

The pattern that stands out is that the two presidents who appointed the most justices — Arroyo (14/15) and Duterte (13/15) — also presided over the lowest dissent rates. The interesting question is why.

There are several theories on how the appointing president and other factors may affect how a justice would vote, and with whom they vote (for example, see studies by Gatmaytan and Magno and Dressel, Inoue and Bonoan). This includes discussions on Filipino cultural traits of gratitude (utang na loob) and a sense of group conformity (pakikisama). Theories on the “freshmen effect” have also been reviewed, wherein a newly appointed justice or judge is more likely to vote in favor of the government of the appointing president.

Implications Beyond the Philippines

Judicial institutions are considered impartial arbiters of the legal questions laid before it. However, Justices are human beings with biases. It could be a cultural trait, life experience, or personal relationship. These factors affect a judge’s or justice’s decision, whether implicitly or explicitly.

The patterns above are drawn from a specific institution in a specific country, but the questions they raise apply to courts almost everywhere. These issues are universal. It may take its own shape depending on the country or region you are examining.

This research is an additional method of looking at legal systems, to provide as much insight into the realities of the application of the law. While my professional experience has been with the Philippine legal system, these methods are grounded in analyses that can be applied to other legal systems.

The Road Ahead

Another direction where this research is heading is community detection. Below is a sample of a complex network graph with community detection represented by the boundary lines versus presidential appointment represented by the color of the nodes.

This is a network graph covering the entire period of 1986 to 2024. The nodes are the justices that held office during the period, and the connections (edges) are the number of times each justice-pair voted together in cases (whether they concurred or dissented together). So, justice-pairs that voted together frequently will have a stronger connection to each other than to other justices.

For a brief background on community detection, this essentially finds groups of nodes that are more connected to each other than those outside their community (for those interested, this uses the Louvain algorithm to detect communities).

An initial look at this graph shows communities resulting from length of tenure together. The plan is to conduct this community detection analysis through 1-year windows (like the dissent rate analysis). Then, we can find cases that show cross-community votes and conduct doctrinal analyses on those cases.

Reflections

I have been asking myself why I was doing this and I came up with two reasons.

First, I wanted to train myself in the new technology. I studied law from 2012 to 2016 and worked as a lawyer since then. This was well within the era of the internet, but prior to AI or large language models, as we know those today. I was trained to work with Philippine legal databases (like CD Asia and Lex Libris), government online databases and even using Google search, as a catch-all last resort. However, I have not worked during the rise of Claude and other LLMs. I took this project to explore what new technology can do and possibly how to incorporate these in legal research or operations. What I captured here is still a small part of what can be explored, but I think I learned a lot from this step.

Second, I wanted to build something that reflects my background. I have spent most of my academic life in science, then spent many more years practicing law. Drawing from my expertise and inspiration from both fields, I took this opportunity to showcase how these two influences can shape an interdisciplinary approach to legal research or operation. This research is that product.

My goal is to bring together science and law. Blending the two has always been a challenge — each field is technical, and excelling in either requires dedication and focus that makes a truly interdisciplinary practice difficult to sustain. With the advent of AI, it becomes easier to combine the two with the ability to apply methodologies from each field into the other, without sacrificing the rigor each discipline requires.

Appendix: Months of Python → 3 weeks with AI

In my law school research, this was done through programming the process step-by-step in Python (for non-coders, this is just the programming language used). This included a (1) web scraper to automatically download cases, (2) a text parser to read the text and identify the parts (e.g., case id, date published, writer of main decisions, votes of the rest of the justices), (3) a program to prepare the identified raw parts into a usable database for modeling, and (4) the quantitative analysis itself, which in the past was creating the adjacency matrix (a table of all the justice pairs and how often they voted together).

I recreated this workflow this time around. However, instead of coding it myself, I decided to test out building this through AI tools. I used Claude Code and Deepseek.

Code Pipeline

All my code is uploaded in GitHub at github.com/kikomobile/SC-Decisions.

The code is disorganized but let me lay out the workflow.

First, 01_scraper.ipynb downloads the Philippine Reports from elibrary.judiciary.gov.ph/philippinereports. These volumes contain cases decided by the Supreme Court of the Philippines for the period specified.

Second, 02_processor.ipynb extracts the text from each volume, performing OCR (optical character recognition) on PDFs that are not searchable.

Third, opening run_ui.bat opens a web-based control panel for the rest of the pipeline. This includes:

  • Parsing the texts of each volume, marking each case and its individual parts (especially the ponente and votes).
  • Extract votes of justices and classify them accordingly.
  • Conduct various analysis including:
    • Interactive Graph View (graph above).
    • Temporal Analysis, including:
      • Dissent Rate Timeline
      • Dissent affinity
      • Bloc deviation
      • Temporal drift
      • Agreement vs Expected
      • Court-wide Dissent Rate
    • Temporal Network (Animated community detection over sliding time windows)

Workflow with Claude Code and Deepseek

In the creation of this pipeline, I started using Claude. I had an idea of the pipeline I wanted to implement, mirroring my old research. So, I used Claude Code to build each step with the overall flowchart mostly in my head.

However, as I progressed, I started running into token limits. I considered upgrading my subscription, but the additional cost was significant (from US$20 to US$100 per month). Instead, I used Deepseek by installing an AI coding agent (called Cline) and connecting my Deepseek account via an API key. I used Claude as a sort of architect, wherein I would prompt it to design tasks that can be pushed to Deepseek for implementation. This limited the use of Claude to design and validate, which for my purposes, fit within the token limits of the Pro plan. The Deepseek API cost me a total of around US$12 for the whole project (including using it to try to correct errors in the text parsing methods).

Learnings from the exercise

Challenges. Text parsing is the most difficult part of the process. The parsing checks the document for specific key words or patterns to identify various parts of the case. For example, in the Philippine Reports, each case starts with the division that decided the cases (“FIRST DIVISION”, “SECOND DIVISION”, “THIRD DIVISION”, or “EN BANC”). The ponente would usually follow the word “DECISION” or “RESOLUTION”. The votes are after the dispositive portion which usually is indicated by the words “SO ORDERED”. Each of these markers needs to be identified by the text parser, to record a new case and the corresponding votes. However, these markers can be affected by the quality of the OCR or encoding process, typographical errors from the cases themselves and the changes in the format of the cases over the years. Some examples of these errors would be detecting capital “I” as 1, or capital “O” as a zero instead. Dirt marks on the scan could be interpreted as a random punctuation like a comma or period. The words “DECISION” or “RESOLUTION” in certain eras of the court were typed out with spaces in between like “D E C I S I O N”. Also, some cases do not have the word “SO ORDERED” or have multiple of them in a single case (as when the case cites the dispositive of another case).

Initially, I tried to let the AI workflow solve these problems directly by providing the raw texts, along with a few notes on what it needs to capture and common pitfalls. However, it could not capture all the possible errors. I had to do sanity checks often and correct the text parser several times.

I ended up creating a separate workflow to improve the detection by manually marking a volume. Using the AI workflow, I created the regex_improve pipeline which has a user interface that can load the raw text of a volume, manually tag the various parts of the text, and save the information into a JSON file. I, then, fed the JSON file into Claude, together with the raw text and the original parser, with instructions to revise the parser to capture the ground truth accurately. This significantly improved the results but was still not perfect. I had to redo this step several times, including introducing a few other checks like matching texts that should contain a justice’s name with a database of all the justices.

This is one example of how I had to intervene in this system to get to a useable result. I had to understand programming, how AI works, how the legal system works, and general knowledge of history to be able to catch these errors and correct them properly. Using AI allowed me to stretch my ability to do this and iterate quickly, but I do not think I would have been able to “vibe code” my way out of it.

Advantages. However, it is an understatement to say AI “stretch[ed] my ability”. I do not have any experience creating a user interface. AI allowed me to create two here. First, the regex_improve pipeline, which allowed me to create a ground truth file in a fraction of the time it would have taken me if I did so manually. Second, the web-based control panel which allows me to iterate through the different pipelines, while tweaking a few variables, to see how this affects the result. While these are not essential for this project, it gave me a tool to iterate and think through each step. It is a tool that I would not have otherwise had if AI wasn’t where it is now.

Not to mention the speed at which I was able to implement this. What took over 6 months of coding, I was able to implement (and iterate several times) this system in just 3 weeks. I was able to create the first table/graph within a week. I took the next week to identify the errors and revise the pipeline. By the end of the second week, I went from detecting 17,732 cases (with around 2,000 of which did not have a ponente or votes) to over 38,000 cases (with less than 500 without a ponente or votes). By the third week, I was iterating on the various computational analyses, including the tweakable variables.

Not only was I able to build out the process faster, but I was also able to extend the scope of my previous research. If I had a question, I could tell Claude to create a separate tab in the control panel and implement the metric I wanted to measure. If I wanted to visualize the data, I would have it create an exportable table or graph. While all of it needs to be checked and validated, the framework can be built quickly — and I look forward to refining it further as this research develops.
